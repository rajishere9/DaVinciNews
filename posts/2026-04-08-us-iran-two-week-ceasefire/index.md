**ISLAMABAD / TEHRAN** — In a stunning reversal of what appeared to be an inevitable descent into total war, the United States and Iran have agreed to a formal two-week ceasefire. The deal, brokered by Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, includes a critical commitment from Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, effectively ending the 40-day blockade that has paralyzed global energy markets.

### The Islamabad Agreement
The breakthrough followed a frantic 48-hour period of indirect negotiations in the Pakistani capital. According to details released by the Pakistani Foreign Ministry, the ceasefire went into effect at 04:00 UTC on Wednesday. 

The agreement's primary components include:
- **Immediate Cessation of Hostilities:** Both nations have agreed to halt all aerial, maritime, and drone-based strikes for a minimum of 14 days.
- **Reopening of the Strait:** Iran has begun the process of removing naval mines and recalling its fast-attack craft from the main shipping channels.
- **Formal Negotiations:** High-level delegations from Washington and Tehran are scheduled to arrive in Islamabad on Friday to begin talks on a more permanent resolution to the conflict.

### A Conflict Reaching Its Limit
The ceasefire comes after a series of high-stakes military escalations that left both nations' strategic reserves strained. Reports early Wednesday indicate that prior to the truce, U.S. attack drones had successfully targeted key command centers in Tehran. Rumors have circulated through regional news agencies regarding the potential death of high-ranking Iranian officials in these strikes, though the Iranian Foreign Ministry has not yet confirmed these reports.

In response to the U.S. strikes, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) had reportedly initiated a final "all-out" retaliatory operation against U.S. facilities across the Middle East just hours before the deal was finalized.

### Global Market Relief
The impact on the global economy was instantaneous. Brent crude, which had been approaching $115 per barrel in anticipation of a total war, plummeted to $94 shortly after the ceasefire announcement. Global equity markets, particularly in Asia and Europe, saw their largest single-day gains in over three years as the threat of an immediate energy shock receded.

### Regional Exceptions and Criticism
While the world breathes a sigh of relief, the ceasefire is not without its detractors. The office of Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu issued a stern statement noting that the U.S.-Iran agreement "does not apply to Lebanon," where Israeli operations against Hezbollah infrastructure continue.

Domestically, Israeli opposition leader Yair Lapid has criticized Netanyahu’s handling of the crisis, labeling the escalation and subsequent U.S.-led truce a "strategic failure" that leaves regional proxy threats unaddressed.

### The 14-Day Window
As the first oil tankers begin their transit through the reopened Strait under joint U.S.-Pakistani monitoring, the international community remains cautiously optimistic. The next two weeks will serve as a critical test of whether this fragile truce can be transformed into a lasting regional stability or if it is merely a pause before a more significant confrontation.
