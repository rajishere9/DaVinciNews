**ISLAMABAD / HOUSTON** — The Pakistani capital is currently under a state of total security lockdown as high-level delegations from the United States and Iran prepare to arrive for what analysts are calling the "last chance" summit. The Friday meeting, intended to solidify a fragile two-week ceasefire, occurs against a backdrop of renewed tensions in the Strait of Hormuz and a historic homecoming for NASA's Artemis II crew.

### Islamabad in Lockdown
Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif’s administration has implemented an extensive security and traffic management plan across Islamabad. A five-star luxury hotel has been cleared of all residents to serve as the exclusive venue for the negotiations. Pakistani paramilitary forces and specialized counter-terrorism units have established multiple perimeters around the site.

The Iranian delegation will be led by Speaker of Parliament Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, a figure known for his deep ties to the IRGC. The United States team is expected to be headed by Vice President James David Vance, marking the highest-level in-person contact between the two nations since the conflict began on February 28.

### The Ceasefire Crisis
The diplomatic push is complicated by a deteriorating situation in the Persian Gulf. Early Thursday, Tehran reportedly halted tanker traffic in the Strait of Hormuz once again, citing continued Israeli strikes in Lebanon as a breach of the "Islamabad Agreement." Shipping data shows a virtual standstill, with only six vessels transiting in the last 24 hours—a fraction of the usual daily average.

Iran’s Ports and Maritime Organisation has issued a stern directive: all vessels must now coordinate directly with Iranian naval forces for transit or risk being "targeted and destroyed." This move has pushed oil prices back up to $97 per barrel, threatening to undo the economic reprieve seen earlier this week.

### Artemis II: The Final Descent
While terrestrial diplomats haggle over corridors and tolls, the four astronauts of Artemis II are preparing for a fiery return to Earth. NASA has confirmed that the Orion spacecraft is on track for a splashdown off the coast of San Diego on Friday, April 10, at approximately 8:07 P.M. EDT.

The mission, which saw the crew shatter the human distance record from Earth, is entering its final critical phase. The Orion capsule is expected to hit the "entry interface" of Earth’s atmosphere at 25,000 miles per hour. Recovery teams, including the USS John P. Murtha, are already in position in the Pacific.

### 24 Hours of Uncertainty
The coming 24 hours will be defined by two disparate but equally historic events: the recovery of the first lunar astronauts of the 21st century and the high-stakes arrival of the Vance and Ghalibaf delegations in Islamabad. Whether the "Celestial Truce" can survive the renewed maritime friction remains the central question for a region—and a world—on edge.
