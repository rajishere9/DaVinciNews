**STRAIT OF HORMUZ / DUBAI** — For the first time in over 40 days, the world’s most critical energy chokepoint has roared back to life. On Wednesday evening, a convoy of six ultra-large crude carriers (ULCCs) successfully completed the transit through the Strait of Hormuz, escorted by a unique joint naval task force of U.S. and Pakistani vessels. The transit marks the most tangible success yet of the newly minted \"Islamabad Agreement.\"

### Breaking the Blockade
The reopening of the Strait was the centerpiece of the two-week ceasefire agreement brokered by Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif. Since 04:00 UTC, Iranian naval forces have been observed withdrawing from the primary shipping lanes and assisting in the verification of \"safe corridors\" for commercial traffic.

While a total of 150 tankers remain in the regional queue, the successful passage of the first six vessels—primarily bound for Asian markets—has sent a powerful signal of stability to the global community. 

### Joint Monitoring: A Fragile Cooperation
The escort mission represents a remarkable moment of localized military cooperation. Under the terms of the Islamabad Agreement, the U.S. and Pakistan are providing primary security for the transit corridors, with Iranian fast-attack craft maintaining a respectful distance within Iranian territorial waters. 

This \"joint escort\" model is intended to prevent any accidental escalations while the formal diplomatic delegations prepare to meet in Islamabad this Friday. Analysts note that the absence of Israeli naval participation in these escorts was a key concession required by Tehran to agree to the reopening.

### Market Impact: Brent Stabilizes at $94
The arrival of the first tankers in the Gulf of Oman had an immediate cooling effect on energy futures. Brent crude, which had spent weeks in triple-digit territory, has stabilized near $94 a barrel. Energy analysts predict that if the 14-day ceasefire holds and the backlog of tankers is cleared, prices could return to pre-war levels of $75-$80 within the month.

### Humanitarian Relief En Route
The reopening is also a critical victory for regional humanitarian efforts. The first three cargo ships carrying life-saving medical supplies for Yemen and Iraq, which had been stranded in Dubai ports for over a month, were among the vessels to transit the Strait today. Aid agencies report that these shipments are critical to addressing the acute shortages caused by the prolonged maritime blockade.

### A Measured Optimism
Despite the day’s successes, the atmosphere in the region remains one of measured optimism. The 14-day clock is already ticking, and the core issues of the conflict—including compensation for war damages and the long-term status of Iran’s nuclear and missile programs—remain unresolved. For tonight, however, the lights of the tankers moving through the Strait provide a much-needed beacon of hope at the dawn of a fragile peace.
