**TEHRAN / JERUSALEM** — The newly minted U.S.-Iran ceasefire, brokered by Pakistan just 24 hours ago, is facing its first major crisis. Early Thursday, Tehran announced the re-closure of the Strait of Hormuz to \"enemy nations,\" citing continued Israeli airstrikes in Lebanon as a direct violation of the spirit of the truce. The move has sent shockwaves through global markets, erasing much of Wednesday's optimistic price drops.

### The Lebanon Exception
The strain on the ceasefire stems from a fundamental disagreement over its scope. While the \"Islamabad Agreement\" established a 14-day pause in direct hostilities between the U.S. and Iran, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu’s office has repeatedly clarified that the truce does not apply to Israeli operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon.

Over the last 24 hours, Israeli forces have expanded their campaign in Lebanon, resulting in hundreds of reported casualties. Tehran, which views Hezbollah as a critical strategic partner, has labeled the ongoing strikes an \"intolerable provocation.\" In response, the Iranian Revolutionary Guard (IRGC) has resumed its blockade of the Strait, stating that freedom of navigation will only be restored when all \"Zionist aggression\" in the region ceases.

### Sea Mines and Tolls
The situation in the Strait is further complicated by reports of increased maritime hazards. Intelligence firms suggest that the IRGC may have deployed fresh sea mines within the Traffic Separation Scheme during the conflict's peak. 

Furthermore, reports have emerged of Iran levying \"reconstruction tolls\" of up to $1 per barrel on outbound oil from regional neighbors. On Wednesday, only 11 vessels were recorded transiting the Strait—a staggering drop from the pre-war daily average of 138.

### Market Reaction: Oil Rebounds
Global markets responded swiftly to the renewed uncertainty. Brent crude, which had plummeted to $94 following the ceasefire announcement, rebounded to $97.02 by 08:00 UTC on Thursday. Asian stock markets also saw broad declines, reflecting a \"cautious mood\" among investors who now fear the 14-day window may close prematurely.

### Islamabad Summit: Still on Track?
Despite the volatility, diplomatic efforts are continuing. Pakistan is still preparing to host high-level delegations from Washington and Tehran this Friday. A 30-member U.S. advance team remains on the ground in Islamabad to oversee security for what is expected to be the first face-to-face negotiation of the conflict. 

U.S. Vice President JD Vance is still anticipated to lead the American team, though the White House has not yet commented on whether the renewed Hormuz blockade will affect the summit's agenda.

### A New Record Above
While tensions simmer on Earth, NASA's Artemis II crew is preparing for their return trajectory after a record-breaking lunar transit. The crew is scheduled for splashdown off the coast of California this Friday, having successfully navigated the furthest point from Earth ever reached by human spaceflight.

As the region teeters between a fragile peace and a return to total war, the coming 24 hours in Islamabad will likely decide the fate of the ceasefire and the long-term stability of the world's most critical energy corridor.
