# Inside-Out Planetary System LHS 1903 Defies Cosmic Rules

In a discovery that turns our understanding of planetary formation literally inside out, an international team of astronomers has identified a system that defies the standard "order of worlds." The system, centered around a dim M-dwarf star known as **LHS 1903**, features large gaseous planets orbiting close to the star, with a small, naked rocky planet lurking further out at the system's edge.

The findings, published today in the journal *Science*, suggest that our long-held models of how solar systems grow—with rocky worlds near the heat and giants in the cold—are far from the only way the universe builds a neighborhood.

### The Standard Model vs. LHS 1903

In our own solar system, and most others observed to date, the pattern is predictable: small, rocky planets like Earth and Mars form in the inner, hotter regions, while gas and ice giants like Jupiter and Saturn form further out where volatile materials like ice and gas are more abundant.

LHS 1903, an ancient red dwarf located roughly 7 billion years old and half the mass of our sun, shatters this convention. NASA’s **TESS** (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite) originally spotted three planets: **LHS 1903 b**, a dense super-Earth, followed by **c** and **d**, two sub-Neptunes with thick gaseous atmospheres. However, when the team used the European Space Agency’s **CHEOPS** (CHaracterising ExOPlanet Satellite) for a deeper look, they found a fourth planet: **LHS 1903 e**.

To the surprise of the researchers, this outermost world is a "naked" rocky core with no atmosphere, located in the very region where gas giants should be bulking up on ice and gas.

### Gas-Depleted Formation: A New Hypothesis

Led by **Thomas Wilson** of the University of Warwick, the team proposed a "gas-depleted formation" mechanism to explain the anomaly. This hypothesis suggests the planets formed sequentially:
1. The inner, gaseous planets formed early while the system was still rich in gas and resources.
2. By the time the outermost planet, LHS 1903 e, began to coalesce, the system's gas had been swept away by stellar winds or other processes.
3. Consequently, the outer world was forced to build itself "pebble by pebble" from the remaining rocky debris, resulting in a rocky planet at the system's edge.

### Why It Matters

"This system is a natural laboratory for understanding the 'radius valley'—the gap that separates smaller rocky worlds from larger gaseous ones," says **Kevin Hardegree-Ullman** of the NASA Exoplanet Science Institute. Because these disparate worlds all orbit the same star, variables like age and composition are constant, allowing scientists to isolate the factors that dictate a planet's final form.

The team's next step involves using the **James Webb Space Telescope** to peer into the atmospheres of the sub-Neptunes, seeking more clues about the resource-rich environment they formed in compared to their lonely, rocky neighbor.
